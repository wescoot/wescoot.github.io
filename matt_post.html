Lane Detection

Teaching a scooter how to see

Following a sidewalk is easy for a human, we know that we simply have to walk between the two edges of the path to avoid getting our feet muddy. For our scooter however, this process is much more involved. The scooter receives video input from the camera mounted on the steering column. An image from the scooter’s camera on a typical path looks like this:

To follow the path in the image, we need to detect the edges of the path so that the scooter knows to stay within those edges. Ideally, we want to find two lines representing the edges of the sidewalk, which look like this:

For a human, this process is easy, but for a computer there are a number of steps that must be taken to estimate the road edges.

After receiving a video frame, the first step is to slightly blur the image to eliminate noise in the image. We use a method known as bilateral filtering which removes noise but preserves edges, such as the sidewalk edge and center line:

The image on the right has been blurred slightly using the bilateral filter. Although the lines are still sharp, the texture of the path is much smoother in the right image, which prevents false positives.

After filtering, the next step is to find the pixels which correspond to edges in the image. We use an algorithm known as Canny edge detection for this step. After applying edge detection, we’re left with something like this

Although the bounds of the road are clearly visible, we also have many lines which are not interesting to us. Specifically, the bridge above the road, and the train on the right are irrelevant to our goal of finding the edges of the sidewalk. Since the camera is mounted in a fixed perspective, we have a general idea of where the edges of the sidewalk are. We can create a region of interest which looks like this and discard the lines which lie outside that region. Notice that we’ve gotten rid of a lot of the useless edges above and to the right of the pathway.

Now that we have some filtered edges, we need to find which of these pixels correspond to lines using Hough transforms. This gives us a list of line segments extracted from the edge pixel image:

We seperate the candidate lane segments into lines which are sloped upwards to the right and those which are sloped upwards to the left. These should correspond to line segments belonging to the left and right lane edges respectively.Using a averaging these two groups, weighted by segment length, we end up with two estimates of the lane boundaries:
